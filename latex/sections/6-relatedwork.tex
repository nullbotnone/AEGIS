\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Zero trust and identity-centric access control.}
Zero trust architectures replace coarse network perimeters with explicit
authentication, authorization, and continuous policy evaluation over users,
services, and resources. NIST SP 800-207 established this model at the system
architecture level~\cite{rose2020zero}, and NIST SP 800-207A extended it to
cloud-native deployments with stronger emphasis on workload identity,
application-level telemetry, and fine-grained enforcement in distributed
services~\cite{duckworth2023spiffe}. \projectname~follows the same continuous-trust
intuition, but shifts the enforcement target from user or service identity to
agent behavior. In our setting, a valid identity is not sufficient: the
relevant question is whether a running agent remains within the behavioral
constraints implied by its authorized HPC task.

\paragraph{LLM-agent security and prompt injection.}
Recent work has shown that LLM-integrated applications and tool-using agents
are vulnerable to indirect prompt injection, tool abuse, and other forms of
instruction-channel confusion. Greshake et al. demonstrated that untrusted
content can steer real LLM-integrated applications by exploiting the mismatch
between data and instructions~\cite{greshake2023not}. Liu et al.
formalized prompt-injection attacks and evaluated attack and defense strategies
across models and tasks~\cite{liu2024formalizing}. ToolEmu and AgentDojo then
provided structured environments for stress-testing tool-using agents and
surfacing safety failures under adversarial conditions~\cite{ruan2023identifying, debenedetti2024agentdojo}.
These efforts are important for characterizing the threat model, but they are
primarily benchmark- and application-oriented. \projectname~addresses a different
problem: how to constrain and verify the behavior of deployed agents at runtime
on a shared cluster, including filesystem, network, and cross-agent effects
that emerge only in multi-tenant execution.

\paragraph{Remote attestation and runtime integrity.}
Remote attestation has traditionally focused on establishing trust in platform
state. Sailer et al. linked attestation to access-control policy
enforcement~\cite{sailer2004attestation}; Haldar et al. proposed semantic
remote attestation for higher-level properties beyond simple code
measurement~\cite{haldar2004semantic}; and PRIMA extended integrity
measurement toward policy-relevant information-flow properties~\cite{jaeger2006prima}.
Kil et al. argued that practical attestation must reason about dynamic runtime
properties rather than only boot-time state~\cite{kil2009remote}, while more
recent work such as ARI targets mission-execution integrity in cyber-physical
and real-time environments~\cite{wang2023ari}. \projectname~is complementary to this
line of work but changes the attested object. Rather than attesting only code
identity, platform state, or timing properties, \projectname~attests whether an AI
agent's observed actions satisfy behavioral constraints derived from its
authorized job, and it extends that reasoning to cluster-wide correlations
across co-located agents.

\paragraph{Data leakage prevention.}
Data leakage prevention systems have been widely studied for data at rest, in
use, and in transit. Shabtai et al. surveyed leakage-detection and prevention
techniques across enterprise channels~\cite{shabtai2012survey}, and Alneyadi et
al. categorized modern DLP systems by their content- and context-analysis
strategies~\cite{alneyadi2016survey}. These systems are effective when leakage can
be recognized from content signatures, protocol context, or policy violations
at a single endpoint or network boundary. However, they are not designed for
the hijacked-agent setting studied here, where the exfiltration channel may be
a legitimate encrypted API and the key violation is behavioral rather than
content-based. In \projectname, the crucial question is not whether a payload matches
a known sensitive pattern, but whether the agent should have performed the
underlying read, write, or network action at all. \projectname~therefore complements
traditional DLP with behavioral attestation and runtime containment rather than
content inspection alone.
