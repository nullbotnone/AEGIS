\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{results}

Our evaluation is organized around the two claim boundaries introduced in
Section~\ref{aegis}. We use \emph{real-path} experiments to measure the implemented
\ebpf-based attestation pipeline on EPYC hardware, and we use
\emph{simulation-based controlled studies} to evaluate broader attack coverage,
component ablation, baseline comparison, and benign-workflow behavior. The
real-path experiments use the deployed \projectname~probe, collector, verifier, and
policy logic; the controlled studies reuse the same policy semantics and attack
families but execute them in a deterministic harness that exposes larger attack
and workload matrices.

\subsection{Methodology}

We evaluate performance on AMD EPYC 7713 64-core processors (2.0\,GHz,
256\,MB L3 cache) with 1536\,GB DDR5 RAM, running Rocky Linux 9.6 with Linux
kernel 5.14.0. This represents realistic HPC compute-node specifications. The
\ebpf-based attestation engine is compiled with \texttt{clang} and runs with
kernel-side syscall interception, while the verifier executes the full \projectname
policy pipeline including behavioral-constraint checking, cross-agent
correlation, and containment decision generation.

Our evaluation separates \emph{real-path measurements} from
\emph{controlled security studies}. The real-path measurements exercise the
implemented probe, collector, and verifier stack on the EPYC platform to
measure direct probe overhead, end-to-end detection latency, and per-attack
behavior. Unless stated otherwise, these experiments use a 1\,s attestation
interval and three repetitions per attack.

The controlled studies use the same policy semantics and attack families, but
execute them in a deterministic harness that exposes larger attack, ablation,
and workload matrices than are convenient to stage on a single node. We use
this harness for the complete attack-coverage study, the specialized-component
ablation, the benign-workflow false-positive study, the scaling analysis, and
the baseline comparison. In the baseline experiment, each defense analyzes the
same attack traces ten times and we report mean analysis time. The benign suite
covers six workflows and 42 benign actions, including multi-agent
collaboration, budget-edge LLM reporting, and tool-heavy post-processing.

\subsection{Real-Path Detection Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:attack-results} summarizes the real-path detection results. \projectname~detects all four implemented attack classes in all real-path trials. At
the default 1\,s attestation interval, the median detection latency is
500.37\,ms for filesystem-mediated injection, 500.14\,ms for co-location
injection, 500.31\,ms for supply-chain injection, and 500.25\,ms for
coordinated exfiltration. These latencies are approximately half of the
attestation interval because the attacks are injected midway through a bundle
window. Median data emitted before detection is 68\,B, 50\,B, 519\,B, and
503\,B, respectively. Median verifier CPU overhead stays below 0.08\% in all
cases.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/attack_results.png}
  \caption{Measured outcomes on the real attestation path at a 1\,s interval.
  Across three trials per attack, \projectname~detects all four attacks with median
  detection latency between 500.14\,ms and 500.37\,ms. Median data emitted
  before containment is 68\,B for filesystem injection, 50\,B for co-location
  injection, 519\,B for supply-chain injection, and 503\,B for coordinated
  exfiltration, while median verifier CPU overhead remains below 0.08\% for
  every attack.}
  \label{fig:attack-results}
\end{figure}

The real-path results also show that \projectname~detects attacks through the intended
policy dimensions rather than through a single catch-all rule. Filesystem
injection triggers denied-path and unauthorized-endpoint violations,
co-location injection is detected through network policy, supply-chain
injection combines sensitive-file access with exfiltration policy, and
coordinated exfiltration triggers both denied-path violations and the
cross-agent covert-channel rule.

\subsection{Controlled Security Coverage}

To complement the real-path measurements, we run the full four-attack suite in
the controlled harness. Table~\ref{tab:controlled-coverage} summarizes these
results. In this setting, all four attacks succeed in hijacking the target
agent and exfiltrating data, and \projectname~detects all four, yielding a 100\%
detection rate over 4/4 completed attacks. The controlled suite produces 16
total findings and 1158\,B of exfiltrated payload across the four attacks.
The simpler filesystem and co-location attacks each trigger one detection,
whereas the supply-chain and coordinated attacks each trigger seven detections
because they violate multiple policy dimensions simultaneously. The coordinated
attack also raises an explicit covert-channel finding, confirming that the
cluster-wide access graph is necessary for this class of multi-agent attack.

These controlled results are important for two reasons. First, they give us a
fully reproducible security-coverage view that is not tied to one hardware
configuration. Second, they show that the real-path results are not accidental:
the same attack classes remain detectable when exercised in a deterministic
harness with the same policy semantics.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:baseline-comparison} compares \projectname~against four representative
alternatives using identical attack traces. Network DLP, filesystem auditing,
and strict sandboxing each detect two of the four attacks (50\%), while
per-agent analytics misses all four. \projectname~is the only defense that detects
every attack class. The richer analysis does incur higher processing cost than
the simpler baselines, but the absolute cost remains small: \projectname~averages
0.081\,ms of analysis time per trace, compared with 0.005--0.010\,ms for the
baselines.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/baseline_comparison.png}
  \caption{Detection outcomes and mean analysis time for five defenses
  evaluated on the same attack traces. Network DLP, filesystem auditing, and
  strict sandboxing each detect only two of four attacks (50\%), while
  per-agent analytics misses all four. \projectname~is the only mechanism that
  detects all attack classes (100\%) and still completes its analysis in
  0.081\,ms on average, compared with 0.005--0.010\,ms for the simpler
  baselines.}
  \label{fig:baseline-comparison}
\end{figure}

These results highlight why \projectname~needs all four policy dimensions. DLP sees
egress but cannot reason about encrypted traffic sent to whitelisted services.
Filesystem auditing sees sensitive paths but misses network-only violations.
Strict sandboxing blocks only the attacks that cross a hard isolation
boundary, and per-agent analytics misses coordinated behavior entirely because
the individual agents remain locally plausible. In contrast, \projectname~combines
constraint checking, signature augmentation, and cross-agent correlation in a
single verifier.

\subsection{Ablation}

The real-path ablation in Figure~\ref{fig:real-ablation} shows that measured
coverage comes from overlapping but non-identical mechanisms. Removing network
policy causes co-location injection to evade detection entirely (0/3), because
that attack is otherwise path-compliant. A permissive profile misses
filesystem, co-location, and supply-chain attacks in all three trials, but it
still detects coordinated exfiltration in all three trials because the
cluster-wide covert-channel rule remains active. Removing only data-access,
tool, or exfil-budget controls does not collapse detection on every attack,
which indicates that the real system benefits from redundancy across
dimensions rather than a single critical check.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/ablation_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Real-path ablation at a 1\,s attestation interval. Full \projectname
  detects all four attacks in all three trials. Removing network policy causes
  the co-location attack to evade detection completely (0/3), while a
  permissive profile misses filesystem, co-location, and supply-chain attacks
  but still detects coordinated exfiltration through cross-agent covert-channel
  correlation. The heatmap also reports the median bytes exfiltrated before
  detection for each configuration.}
  \label{fig:real-ablation}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:simulated-ablation} sharpens this observation by isolating the specialized
detection mechanisms. In the simulation harness, full \projectname~detects all four
controlled attacks, and removing any one of the four specialized mechanisms
drops detection from 100\% to 75\% by allowing exactly its matching attack
class to pass. A minimal path-only policy detects none of the attacks. Taken
together, the real and simulated ablations support the intended interpretation:
the constraint layer provides the primary security boundary, while specialized
checks such as covert-channel correlation and injection signatures add
orthogonal coverage.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/simulated_ablation_breakdown.png}
  \caption{Controlled simulated ablation isolating the four specialized
  detection mechanisms. Full \projectname~detects all four attack classes, and
  removing any one mechanism drops overall detection to 75\% by allowing
  exactly the matching attack class to evade detection: no volume limits misses
  the volume attack, no sensitive-file detection misses the sensitive-file
  attack, no covert-channel logic misses the covert-channel attack, and no
  injection signatures misses the supply-chain attack. A minimal path-only
  policy detects none of the attacks.}
  \label{fig:simulated-ablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Performance}

Figure~\ref{fig:performance-overhead} combines the two most important
performance views. First, the direct probe microbenchmarks quantify worst-case
overhead when a single syscall family is exercised continuously. File-centric
syscalls are the most expensive: elapsed-time overhead is 23.3\% for
\texttt{openat} and 28.2\% for \texttt{read}, while network connection
monitoring adds 5.35\% for \texttt{connect}. These microbenchmarks are
deliberately pessimistic and should be interpreted as upper bounds on probe
cost rather than application-level slowdown.

Second, the real end-to-end latency sweep shows that the operational overhead
of \projectname~on the full verifier path is substantially lower. All four attacks
remain detectable across the entire tested range from 100\,ms to 10\,s.
Detection latency tracks the attestation interval, rising from 50.3\,ms at a
100\,ms interval to 500.2\,ms at 1\,s and 5.00\,s at 10\,s. Over the same
interval sweep, verifier CPU overhead drops from 0.588\% to 0.051\% and then
to 0.0052\%. At the default 1\,s interval, the system therefore achieves
sub-second detection with approximately 0.05\% measured verifier CPU
overhead.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/performance_overhead.png}
  \caption{Combined performance summary from the real measurements. The left
  panel shows direct probe overhead in worst-case syscall microbenchmarks:
  elapsed-time overhead is 23.3\% for \texttt{openat}, 28.2\% for
  \texttt{read}, and 5.35\% for \texttt{connect}. The right panel shows
  end-to-end detection latency and verifier CPU overhead versus attestation
  interval; latency scales from 50.3\,ms at 100\,ms intervals to 500.2\,ms at
  1\,s and 5.00\,s at 10\,s, while CPU overhead falls from 0.588\% to 0.051\%
  and 0.0052\%, respectively.}
  \label{fig:performance-overhead}
\end{figure}

We also ran a secondary simulation-based scaling study to explore regimes that
are inconvenient to measure directly on the single-node EPYC setup. As shown
in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-sweep}, 11 of 16 tested configurations remain below
5\% overhead. At a 1\,s attestation interval, the mixed workload incurs 1.88\%
overhead for 10 agents, 3.67\% for 50 agents, 9.38\% for 100 agents, and
25.36\% for 500 agents. Workload sensitivity is dominated by I/O intensity,
ranging from 0.63\% for compute-heavy jobs to 8.87\% for I/O-heavy jobs. We
report these numbers as a controlled scaling trend rather than as a deployment
measurement.

\subsection{Benign Workloads}

Figure~\ref{fig:false-positive-summary} summarizes the benign-workflow study. The expanded benign-workflow suite produces no observed false positives. Across
six workflows and 42 benign actions, \projectname~reports zero alerts. The suite is
not limited to trivially clean traces: it includes standard single-agent HPC
pipelines, authorized multi-agent collaboration, budget-edge LLM reporting,
and tool-heavy post-processing, covering both workflow size and the absence of
alerts.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/false_positive_summary.png}
  \caption{Expanded benign-workflow suite used to assess false positives. The
  left panel reports workflow coverage in terms of executed actions and logged
  events across six benign workflows, including authorized multi-agent
  collaboration, budget-edge LLM reporting, and tool-heavy postprocessing. The
  right panel shows that \projectname~raises no false positives in any workflow,
  yielding 0 detections across 42 benign actions.}
  \label{fig:false-positive-summary}
\end{figure}

We emphasize the scope of this claim carefully: the result shows that no false
positives were observed on the evaluated benign suite, not that false positives
are impossible. Nevertheless, the combination of zero observed false positives
and the broadened workflow coverage suggests that declarative behavioral
constraints can remain precise enough for practical HPC agent workloads.
